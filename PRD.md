# PRD — Studer Equipment Liquidation Playbook

**Owner:** [Machinist's name]
**Date:** May 2026
**Goal:** Sell off a warehouse of used/rebuilt Studer cylindrical grinders, CNC machines, parts, and supplies — with the least possible effort from the owner, who is a craftsman, not a salesperson.

---

## 1. The simple version (read this first)

There are only **three paths** to sell this stuff. Pick one and stop worrying about the others.

| Path | What you do | What you get | Time | Effort |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **A. One phone call** | Call a liquidator. They drive out, look at everything, write a check for the whole lot. | ~25–40% of fair market value | 2–6 weeks | Lowest |
| **B. One auction** | Hire an auction house. They photograph, market, run a one-day online auction. You get a check minus their commission. | ~50–70% of fair market value | 6–12 weeks | Low |
| **C. Sell it yourself, piece by piece** | List the big machines on dealer sites, work parts on eBay, take calls. | 70–100% of fair market value | 6–18 months | Highest |

**Recommendation for someone who is not a salesperson:** Do **Path B (auction)** for everything. It is the best balance of money-for-effort. If you already know one or two machines are extra valuable (newer Studer CNCs, S40 with low hours, etc.), pull those out and try Path C **only on those few**, then auction the rest.

If you want to be done in 30 days and don't care about getting top dollar, do **Path A**.

The rest of this document gives you the exact phone calls to make for each path, in order.

---

## 2. Background — why this is hard, and what makes it easier

Studer is a top-tier Swiss precision grinder brand (United Grinding group). Used Studers hold value better than almost any other grinder line. There is real, ongoing demand from:

- Aerospace, medical, automotive, and tool & die shops doing OD/ID grinding
- Independent grinder rebuilders who fix them up and resell
- Export buyers (Mexico, India, Turkey, Eastern Europe) — these are 30–50% of buyers of older Studers

**What makes this easier than selling a generic CNC mill:**
- The brand sells itself. You don't need to convince anyone Studer is good.
- A small number of dealers handle most of the used-Studer market in North America. You only need to talk to ~10 people total.
- Auction buyers know what a Studer is worth and will bid against each other.

**What makes it harder:**
- Buyers want to see machines run under power. If they aren't powered up and producing a good part, you lose ~30% of value.
- Rigging a Studer is heavy and specialized. Buyer normally pays, but you need ground-level loading or a forklift.
- A pile of "parts" with no inventory list is worth almost nothing. A photographed, listed pile of parts is worth real money.

## 3. Inventory — do this before you call anyone

Before any sale path, you need a list. Without a list you can't get a quote, you can't list anything, and dealers won't take you seriously. **This is the hardest single step. Do it once, do it well, and the rest is easy.**

### 3.1 The "one-page-per-machine" rule

For every whole machine, fill out one page (paper or spreadsheet — see `inventory-template.csv` in this repo). On that page:

1. **Make:** Studer (or other)
2. **Model:** S20, S30, S33, S40, S145, S150, etc. — read it off the nameplate
3. **Serial number:** off the nameplate
4. **Year of manufacture:** off the nameplate, or estimate
5. **Control:** Manual / Studer Granit / Fanuc 0i / Fanuc 31i / Siemens 840D / etc.
6. **Swing & between centers:** the size envelope
7. **Powered up? Yes / No** — and if yes, when last
8. **Condition:** "rebuilt by me in [year], runs production parts" / "as-is, last ran [year]" / "needs work"
9. **Accessories included:** wheel head(s), tailstock, steady rests, chucks, dressers, fixtures, manuals, electrical schematics
10. **Photos:** at least 6 — full machine, control panel, nameplate, wheel head close-up, tailstock, accessories laid out
11. **Video:** 30-second walk-around. If powered, 30 seconds of it cycling.
12. **Your asking price:** see Section 7
13. **Walk-away price:** the lowest number you'll take

### 3.2 Parts and supplies

You will not write one page per part. Instead:

1. **Sort everything into bins** by category: spindles, wheelheads, motors, drives/controls, hydraulics, way covers, chucks/centers, grinding wheels, dressers, hand tools, gauges, fixtures, fasteners, scrap.
2. **Photograph each bin** and label it (Bin 1, Bin 2, …).
3. **Make one line in a spreadsheet per bin:** Bin number, category, rough quantity, photo file name, asking price for the whole bin.
4. For Studer-branded parts (spindles, wheelheads, controls): list these **individually** with serial/part number if you can find one. They are worth real money and should not be sold by the bin.

### 3.3 The Big List

When you're done, you should have:
- One spreadsheet line per **whole machine** (~however many — likely 5–30 lines)
- One spreadsheet line per **Studer-branded part of significance** (~20–100 lines)
- One spreadsheet line per **bin of misc parts/supplies** (~20–60 lines)

That spreadsheet is "The Big List." Every dealer call you make starts with: *"I have a warehouse of Studer equipment. I have a Big List with photos. Can I email it to you?"* That single sentence will get you taken seriously.

**You want:** somebody to come look, write a check, and haul it away.

### Step 2. Wait for offers

You will get 3–6 responses within a week. Most will say "send more photos," some will want to visit. **Do not accept the first offer.** Tell each one: *"I have other quotes coming in. What's your best number?"*

### Step 3. Pick the best offer, sign a one-page bill of sale

Use the bill of sale template in `bill-of-sale-template.md`. Key clauses:
- **As-is, where-is**, no warranty
- **Buyer pays for rigging and removal**
- **Wire transfer only**, in your bank by the day before pickup
- **No items leave the building until funds are confirmed in your account by your banker**

### Step 4. Be there when they load out

A liquidator pulling a whole shop will spend 1–3 days hauling. Be there. Watch what goes on the truck. Anything you wanted to keep should already be moved out before they show up.

**Total time, Path A: 3–6 weeks. Total dollar: ~25–40% of full retail.**

## 5. Path B — One auction (recommended for most sellers)

**You want:** a professional to handle photography, marketing, and the sale. You get a check 30 days after the gavel drops.

### Step 1. Email the Big List to 3 auction houses

Same email as Path A but with subject *"Auction inquiry — full Studer grinder shop"*.

| Auction House | What they do | Website |
|---|---|---|
| **Hilco Industrial / Hilco Global** | The biggest. Will take a single shop or run a multi-seller event. | hilcoglobal.com |
| **Heritage Global Partners** | Major industrial auctioneer. Strong in machine tools. | hgp.com |
| **Maynards Industrial** | Industrial-focused, big in machine tools, North American + international reach. | maynards.com |
| **Tauber-Arons** (Centurion Service Group) | Long-running machine-tool auctioneer, heavy on the West Coast. | centurionservice.com |
| **Tiger Group** | Asset disposition; runs online machinery auctions regularly. | tigergroup.com |
| **Liquidity Services / AllSurplus** | Online-only auctions; runs constantly; lower minimums. | allsurplus.com |
| **Perry Process** (process equipment heavy, but takes machine tools) | Established auction house, strong export reach. | perryprocess.com |

Three is enough. You don't need more. Pick: **one big national** (Hilco or Heritage), **one machine-tool specialist** (Maynards or Tauber-Arons / Centurion), and **one online-first** (Tiger or AllSurplus).

### Step 2. Compare the proposals on three numbers

Each will send a proposal. Compare on:

1. **Commission rate to the seller** — standard is 10–15% from the seller, plus a "buyer's premium" of 15–18% the buyer pays on top of the hammer. Sometimes the seller commission is 0 if the buyer's premium is high enough — read carefully.
2. **Marketing budget / reserve policy** — do they let you set reserves (minimum bids)? On a Studer, you should be able to. *Walk away from any auctioneer who says "no reserves."*
3. **Their published list of past Studer sales** — ask: *"Send me the hammer prices on the last 5 Studers you sold."* The good ones will. That's your reality check.

### Step 3. Sign with one auction house

Their contract will cover photography, advertising, online platform, payment processing, and pickup coordination with buyers. **Read the section on what happens to no-sale items** — usually they go back to you, sometimes there's a "passed lot" fee.

### Step 4. Stay out of the way

The auctioneer will send a photographer for 1–2 days. Then there's a 3–6 week marketing window, then a 1-day online auction event, then a 5–10 day removal window. You collect the check 30–45 days after the auction.

**Total time, Path B: 8–14 weeks. Total dollar: ~50–70% of full retail.**

## 6. Path C — Sell it yourself (highest dollar, hardest work)

**You want:** to get full retail. You can spend hours per machine answering questions, hosting visits, and shipping parts.

Do this **only** for the top 2–5 most valuable machines. Auction the rest. Trying to sell 30 machines piece-by-piece will burn you out.

### Step 1. List big machines on dealer-network sites

These are the sites where dealers and end-users actually look. List on **all of them** — listing is usually free or cheap for sellers, and dealers cross-list to all of them anyway.

| Site | What it is | How it works |
|---|---|---|
| **MachineTools.com** | The biggest used-machinery directory in North America. | Free seller listings (basic). Paid upgrades. Dealers comb this daily. |
| **Surplus Record** | 100-year-old industrial classifieds. Print + online. | Paid listing, low cost, strong export reach. |
| **Machinio** | Aggregator — pulls from dealer sites. End-users search here. | List directly or it'll find you if you list on MachineTools.com. |
| **EquipNet** | Asset management + brokerage. Listings + their broker network. | Free listing; they'll also offer brokerage. |
| **Locator Online** (Hilco-owned) | Long-standing dealer network. | Paid listing, dealer-heavy. |
| **eBay Industrial** | Real auction with global reach. Best for **smaller accessories and parts**, not whole machines (rigging scares eBay buyers). | Standard eBay fees. |
| **Practical Machinist Classifieds** | The forum where shop owners hang out. | Free posting in the For Sale section. End-user buyers, not dealers. |
| **Exapro** | European/international platform. Worth listing for export reach. | Free basic listing. |

### Step 2. Listing copy template

Use this exact format. Buyers read 50 listings a day; clarity wins.

```
[Year] STUDER [MODEL] CNC/Manual Universal Cylindrical Grinder
S/N: [serial]
Control: [Fanuc 31i / Studer Granit / Manual]
Swing: [mm or in] · Between centers: [mm or in]
Hours: [if known, else "unknown"]
Condition: Fully rebuilt in [year] by owner (machinist, [N] years on Studers).
Last produced production parts in [month/year].
Powered, can be demonstrated under power on site.

Includes:
  - [list every accessory]
  - Original manuals and electrical schematics
  - [any tooling]

Asking: $[number] OBO. Buyer responsible for rigging and freight.
Located in [city, state]. Loading: [forklift / dock / ground level].

Contact: [name, phone, email]. Serious inquiries only.
```

### Step 3. Screen buyers — do this every time

You will get scam emails. Common scams:
- "I'll send a cashier's check for more than the price, you wire the difference to my shipper." **Always a scam. Hang up.**
- "I'm overseas, I'll have my freight forwarder pick up after I send a deposit." Sometimes legit. **Demand wire transfer in full to your bank, confirmed by your banker, before anything moves.**
- "Can you ship to [country]? Here's my freight account." Legit-ish. Make them pay first.

**Three rules, no exceptions:**
1. Wire transfer only. No checks, no PayPal, no Zelle, no "escrow service" they suggest.
2. Funds confirmed by your banker (not by an email screenshot) before anything leaves the building.
3. Bill of sale signed before pickup. Buyer arranges and pays the rigger.

### Step 4. Parts and supplies — work in parallel with the big-machine listings

For parts, work them in this order:

1. **Studer-branded parts (spindles, wheelheads, controls, hydraulic units):** post on Practical Machinist classifieds and eBay. List individually with photos and part numbers. These can be hundreds to thousands of dollars each.
2. **Fanuc / Siemens / Allen-Bradley electronic parts:** these dealers buy them outright — get a quote first:
   - **Classic Automation** — classicautomation.com
   - **Radwell International** — radwell.com
   - **PLC Center** — plccenter.com
3. **Precision measuring (gauges, indicators, surface plates, micrometers):** eBay or local calibration shops. **Long Island Indicator Service** (longislandindicator.com) buys some used metrology.
4. **Grinding wheels, dressers, diamond rolls:** sell as a lot to a local grinding shop or list on Practical Machinist. New-in-box wheels resell well; used wheels are scrap.
5. **General tooling (chucks, centers, steady rests, fixtures):** list as lots on eBay or sell to a local tooling reseller (Penn Tool, Travers — they don't always buy, but they may broker).
6. **Hand tools, fasteners, miscellaneous shop:** estate-sale style — one big "shop cleanout" lot on Facebook Marketplace or Craigslist, or include in the auction.
7. **Anything that doesn't sell in 60 days:** scrap it. Cast iron, copper wire, and steel have real value at the scrap yard. Call a local scrap dealer — most will pick up for free if the load is big enough.

## 7. Pricing — what's a Studer worth?

You need a number to ask. Here is how to get one without paying for an appraisal.

### 7.1 Three free comps in 30 minutes

For each machine, do this:

1. Go to **machinetools.com**, search the model. Note the asking prices. Asking prices run **15–30% above sold prices**.
2. Go to **machinio.com**, same search. More listings, same logic.
3. Email two used-Studer dealers (see Section 8) the photos and ask: *"What would you pay me for this machine, as-is, picked up?"* Their offer is roughly **wholesale** (40–60% of retail asking price). Your true fair-market-value is between their offer and the asking-price comps.

### 7.2 Rough Studer model price bands (2026, USD, rebuilt and running)

These are **rules of thumb** for setting a starting asking price. Your actual realized price will depend heavily on year, control, hours, and accessories.

| Model | Type | Era | Typical asking price band |
|---|---|---|---|
| Older S-series (S20, S30) manual | OD / universal | 1970s–80s | $8k–$25k |
| S20/S21 ID grinders | Internal | 1980s–90s | $15k–$45k |
| S30/S31 universal | OD/ID | 1990s–2000s | $25k–$70k |
| S33 universal CNC | OD/ID | 2000s | $40k–$110k |
| S40 universal CNC | OD/ID | 2000s–2010s | $75k–$220k |
| S145 / S150 production ID | Internal CNC | 2000s–2010s | $40k–$130k |
| Newer S22 / S31 / S41 with current Fanuc/Siemens | CNC | 2015+ | $120k–$350k+ |

**These are starting points only.** A rebuild documentation package (yours) and a runnable demo bumps the band by 15–25%. Missing accessories, no power-up, or no manuals drop it 25–40%.

### 7.3 When to pay for an appraisal

If the warehouse total looks like more than ~$500k, a USPAP (formal) appraisal from a member of the **Machinery Dealers National Association (MDNA, mdna.org)** or **Association of Machinery and Equipment Appraisers (AMEA, amea.org)** is worth the $1,500–$3,500 it costs. It gives you a defensible number for negotiations and for your accountant at year-end.

## 9. Logistics — rigging, shipping, who pays

**The rule everyone in the industry follows: buyer pays for rigging and freight.** You do not need to arrange it. You just need to:

1. Be honest about the loading situation in the listing: **dock-height, ground-level, or upper floor?** Forklift available? Floor weight rating?
2. Have the machine **clear, accessible, and disconnected** before pickup. No removing yourself — that's the rigger's job — but make sure power is locked out and air/coolant lines are drained.
3. Schedule a 2-hour load window with the rigger. Be there.
4. Ask the buyer to send you the rigger's COI (Certificate of Insurance) before they show up. Real riggers have this. Scammers don't.

**For reference, here are common machine-tool riggers** (the buyer hires them; you don't pay):
- North American Rigging — nationaria-rigging.com / regionals
- Stevens Rigging
- Allegiance Rigging
- Scott-Macon Equipment (Houston)
- Pedowitz Machinery Movers (Northeast)

If you are doing Path A or B, the buyer/auctioneer handles all of this. You have nothing to do.

## 10. Legal & money — the short version

You are not opening a complicated business. Three documents handle 95% of it:

1. **Bill of Sale** — see `bill-of-sale-template.md`. One page. As-is, where-is, no warranty, buyer rigs.
2. **Resale certificate from the buyer** — if the buyer is a dealer, they will give you a state resale certificate so you don't collect sales tax. File it.
3. **1099 / Schedule C from your accountant** — talk to your CPA *before* the first sale, not after. Especially for a large liquidation, the tax handling matters (capital gains vs ordinary income depending on how the equipment is on the books).

**Do not sell to a buyer until cleared funds are in your account.** Wire fraud and counterfeit cashier's checks are the #1 way machine-tool sellers get burned. No exceptions.

## 11. The recommended order of operations (the "do this on Monday" list)

If you do nothing else, do these steps in this order. Each step is one small task, not a project.

### Week 1 — Inventory only

- [ ] Day 1–2: Walk the warehouse with a clipboard. Make one row per machine in the spreadsheet.
- [ ] Day 3: Photograph each machine — 6 photos each.
- [ ] Day 4: Power up the 5 machines you can power up. Take a 30-second video of each.
- [ ] Day 5: Sort loose parts/supplies into bins. Photograph each bin.

**Result of Week 1:** The Big List exists. You can email it.

### Week 2 — Email everyone

- [ ] Day 1: Email the Big List to all 6 liquidators in Section 4 (Path A bidders).
- [ ] Day 2: Email the Big List to 3 auction houses in Section 5 (Path B bidders).
- [ ] Day 3: Email the Big List to the 5 Studer specialists in Section 8 — ask for their best offer on individual machines.
- [ ] Day 4: Email parts dealers (Classic Automation, Radwell, PLC Center) the relevant subset for control/electrical parts.
- [ ] Day 5: Triage the responses into a simple table: who, what they want, what they offered.

**Result of Week 2:** You have ~10 actual offers in front of you.

### Week 3 — Decide

- [ ] Look at Week 2's offers. If a liquidator's number is close to what an auction estimate would net you after commission, take Path A and be done. If it isn't, sign with the best auction house.
- [ ] Pull out 2–5 individual machines that a Studer specialist made a strong individual offer on. Sell those direct to that specialist.

### Weeks 4–12 — Execute

- [ ] Liquidator or auctioneer handles the bulk.
- [ ] You answer 2–3 emails a week.
- [ ] At the end, scrap whatever didn't sell.

**That's the entire process. Twelve weeks, three real decisions.**

## 13. Success criteria

This project is "done" when:

- The warehouse is empty (or down to whatever the seller wants to keep).
- He has banked the money from the sale.
- He didn't get scammed.
- He didn't have to learn how to be a salesperson.

**Best-case outcome:** 60–75% of full retail, in 90 days, via auction.
**Acceptable outcome:** 35% of full retail, in 30 days, via one-call liquidation.
**Worst case (avoid):** machines sit for 2 years, slowly losing value as buyers move on to newer technology.

The right answer for almost anyone in this position is **"take the auction route, sleep well at night."**
